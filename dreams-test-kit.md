# Vazhi Dream Explorer — User Test Kit

A field-ready script for validating **what students and parents understand** after using the prototype. Not a UX audit — a comprehension test. ~10 minutes per person.

> The transition this kit serves: from "is the design good?" (design validation) to **"what does a real person understand after using it?"** (product validation).

---

## Before you start
- Open `dreams.html` **on a phone**, not a laptop. The target user is mobile.
- Hand them the phone. Say: *"Imagine you're deciding what to do after Class 12. Look around and think out loud — there's no right answer, and I can't help you."*
- **Then stay silent.** Don't guide, don't explain, don't rescue. Every time you want to help, that's a finding.

## Watch silently (don't prompt) — 3 behavioural signals
1. **Where do they stop?** Do they stay in the colourful career-discovery layer, or move to "Show me the paths"?
2. **Do they open an unknown-career snapshot** (Geologist, etc.)? Do they tap "See this path →" inside it, or close it?
3. **Where's the first moment of confusion or a hard word** they skip over or misread?

Jot the moment they *first* look stuck, and what they were looking at.

---

## The 3 questions (ask AFTER they've explored ~5 min)

**Q1 — "What is this website trying to teach you?"**
- ✅ Pass: *"There are many paths / ways to reach the same goal."*
- ⚠️ Partial: *"Lots of careers / lots of colleges."* → it's teaching careers, not dreams (see structural note below).
- ❌ Fail: *"I'm not sure."*

**Q2 — "What surprised you?"**
- ✅ Pass: *"I didn't know [career/institution] existed"* — horizon expanded.
- ❌ Concern: nothing surprised them → the discovery layer isn't landing.

**Q3 — "If your first plan doesn't work out, what would you do?"** *(the most important — this is the counselling philosophy)*
- ✅ Pass: *"I have alternatives / backups."*
- ❌ Fail: *"I don't know"* / *"give up"* / *"try the same exam again."*

**Q4 — "Before today, what did you think about government colleges? Has that changed?"** *(tests the institution belief-shift — a core differentiator)*
- ✅ Pass: *"I thought private was better,"* / *"I didn't know these govt colleges existed,"* / *"I didn't know the fees were so different,"* / *"I didn't know govt colleges had these opportunities."*
- ❌ Concern: no shift — they still equate *good career = expensive private college*. That belief is exactly what Vazhi exists to change.

### ★ The one signal that matters most — "In one sentence, what is Vazhi?"

After everything, ask them to explain Vazhi in a single sentence. **This is the single most important observation in the whole test** — it tells you whether the *positioning* landed, unprompted.

- ✅ Nailed it: something like *"It shows different ways to reach the same goal, and helps me find good affordable colleges."* (paths **+** affordability, in their own words)
- ⚠️ Partial: only one half — *"It shows career options"* (paths, no affordability) or *"It's about cheap govt colleges"* (affordability, no paths).
- ❌ Missed: a generic *"a career website"* / *"it has college info."*

If you start hearing the ✅ version *naturally*, across people, you've nailed the positioning — and that's worth more than any single click metric.

**The one-line verdict per person:** did they leave with **options + a sense of what to do next**, or **options but no next step**? Only the second is a failure (a Class 10 leaving with just "options, decide later" is a *success*).

## Gate 0 — test this FIRST, before any group

**The very first thing to learn: can a true first-timer understand the homepage with zero explanation?**

You are now deep in the product — dreams, paths, institutions, backups are obvious *to you*. A new student has none of that. Before the structured groups, sit anyone who has never seen Vazhi in front of the homepage and ask, before they touch anything:

> *"Just looking at this screen — what do you think this website is for?"*

- ✅ If they get the gist ("it helps me figure out what to do after school / find paths to a career") → the foundation holds; the rest is refinement.
- ❌ If they can't → **everything else is secondary.** Fix the homepage's first-impression before running the full test.

---

## Run it with 4 groups (priority order)

| # | Group | n | What this group specifically tells you |
|---|-------|---|----------------------------------------|
| **1** | **Tamil-medium students** | 3 | **Highest risk.** Is the meaning graspable in 3 seconds, or do English career titles block comprehension? Watch Q-words like "Epidemiologist." |
| 2 | English-medium govt-school students | 3 | Baseline comprehension + does "many paths" land (Q1)? |
| 3 | **Parents** (separately!) | 3 | **Different success metric:** does the **₹2L vs ₹60L** affordability contrast land? Ask them: *"What's the most useful thing here?"* |
| 4 | Students who recently missed NEET/JEE | 2 | Does the **backup-path philosophy** actually console + redirect? Watch the "What if I don't clear NEET?" moment + Q3. |

**Parents are tested separately and judged differently.** Student success = "I learned my options." Parent success = "I learned this is affordable / there's a cheaper strong route." Don't average them.

## Simple recording sheet (one row per person)

| Person | Group | **First thing clicked** | Stopped at (discovery / paths / detail) | Opened a snapshot? | First confusion | Q1 | Q2 | Q3 | Q4 | Left with next-step? |
|--------|-------|-------------------------|----------------------------------------|--------------------|-----------------|----|----|----|----|---------------------|
| | | | | | | | | | | |

> **"First thing clicked" is one of the most revealing columns.** A student tapping a dream is normal; a student tapping "I already know" tells you they arrive with intent; a parent tapping "What will it cost?" first confirms affordability is their entry concern. The pattern across people tells you which audience the homepage actually serves.

### Also capture — the spontaneous mention (the highest-value note)

After each session, write down, **verbatim**, the most memorable thing the person said *without being asked*:

> Person 03 (parent): *"I knew AIIMS was good, but I never realised the fee difference was this big."*
> Person 07 (student): *"There are many alternatives to MBBS."*
> Person 11 (student): *"Geologist sounds interesting."*

The word **spontaneously** matters. People answer Q1–Q4 because you asked them to. The thing they *volunteer* — unprompted — is often the truest signal of what landed. Keep a running list across all 13 people; recurring volunteered phrases are gold.

---

## After the interviews — analyse for insight, not usage

**Don't optimise for the average. Hunt for surprises.** The average result confirms what you expected; the outlier reveals what Vazhi uniquely solves.

- *10 students say "many paths to the same dream"* — good, expected, confirms the core.
- *2 students say "I didn't know Government Nursing Colleges existed"* — **more valuable.** A specific information gap Vazhi is uniquely closing.
- *A parent says "I knew AIIMS was good, but never realised how large the fee difference was"* — **gold.** That is the exact belief shift the product exists to create.

Tally the **insights people leave with**, not the pages they opened. After the first ~15 interviews, fill this:

| Insight | Count |
|---------|-------|
| Learned there are multiple paths to one goal | |
| Discovered a new **career** they'd never heard of | |
| Discovered a new **institution** they'd never heard of | |
| Changed their view of government colleges | |
| Identified a concrete **backup plan** | |
| Could explain Vazhi in one sentence (the ★ signal) | |
| ⚠️ Still believes private is better | |

This table will tell you more about Vazhi than another design review. The bottom row is the one to watch fearfully — every tally there is someone the core message didn't reach.

> **A caution for yourself:** you currently believe the *unknown-careers* section is the star. Students might actually dwell in *"Why this institution?"*, and parents might spend all their time on affordability. Let the table tell you where attention really goes — don't assume.

## How to read the results

- **If Q1 answers cluster on "many careers" not "many paths"** → the discovery layer is teaching careers before dreams. Candidate fix: reframe the cluster header ("Different ways to help people"), and/or surface the "other paths to the same dream" idea *earlier*. Confirm with the data first.
- **If Tamil-medium students stall on career names** → the title needs to carry meaning, not the explanation. Candidate fix: plain-language-first labels ("Disease Detective · *Epidemiologist*"). A/B both.
- **If parents light up at the fee contrast** → that's the lead proposition; make it reachable from parent mode (currently buried in path detail).
- **If missed-NEET students relax at the backup tiers** → the core philosophy works; protect it.
- **If Q4 shows no belief-shift on government colleges** → the institution message isn't landing. Candidate fixes: make "Why this institution?" more prominent, and lead with the fee contrast. This is the hardest belief to move and the biggest differentiator — weight it heavily.

The goal is not to pass. The goal is to find the *one or two* places where what you intended and what they understood diverge — and fix only those.
